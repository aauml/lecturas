<Fragment set:html={`
<p class="lede">Mathias Risse, the moral philosopher who directs Harvard's Carr-Ryan Center for Human Rights, has written what amounts to a structural diagnosis of the global AI governance problem. Three conditions, he argues, are jointly necessary for AI regulation to genuinely serve human rights: <strong>governance reach</strong>, the practical capacity to enforce rules over the actors that build and deploy the systems; <strong>technological power</strong>, a meaningful concentration of frontier development within the regulating jurisdiction; and <strong>rights commitment</strong>, a credible, institutionally embedded dedication to privacy, non-discrimination, due process, and human dignity that functions as a genuine constraint on both state and market. Each of the three major regulatory blocs in the world — China, the United States, the European Union — has, on Risse's reading, at most two. The missing condition, in each case, is the one that turns the rest into something other than what it claims to be.</p>

  <p>What follows reads Risse's commentary as it stands, with attention to the parts of his argument that have already moved since he wrote it on 22 April. The trilogue stall in Brussels has resolved. The NIST contraction has continued. The window he describes as "open now" is, by his own logic, narrower today than two weeks ago.</p>

  <a class="callback" href="../../ai-act-trilogue-stalled/en/">
    <strong>Continues themes from N° 03</strong> &nbsp;·&nbsp; What N° 03 covered as the procedural breakdown of the EU's Digital Omnibus negotiation — twelve hours in Brussels ending at two in the morning — Risse names as the EU's structural condition: rights commitment without industrial leverage. The trilogue has since reached provisional agreement; the trilemma has not.
  </a>

  <span class="section-number">§ 01 — The framework</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">A philosopher's <em>trilemma</em></h2>
  <p class="section-standfirst">Risse is a German-born moral philosopher and the Berthold Beitz Professor in Human Rights, Global Affairs and Philosophy at Harvard. His argument is normative before it is descriptive: the question is not which AI regime is most efficient, or most innovative, but which can plausibly serve human rights. Three legs, one stool. Take one away, the rest is something else.</p>

  <p>The three conditions are jointly necessary, Risse argues, "for AI regulation to genuinely serve human rights." Governance reach without rights commitment "produces enforced repression." Technological power without governance "produces unaccountable systems whose harms are distributed invisibly across populations." Rights commitment without technological power "produces principled regulation of systems the regulator does not build and cannot fully steer." Each formulation is doing work: it identifies what each missing leg actually destroys.</p>

  <p>Strictly, this is not a logical trilemma — Risse acknowledges as much. A trilemma in the technical sense is a constraint where all three goals cannot in principle be satisfied together. The three conditions he names <em>can</em> be satisfied together; one can imagine, for each of the three blocs, the concrete reforms that would make them so. The reason he uses the word anyway is political. These three regimes are the only globally significant regulatory models that exist, the configuration is stable, and from the standpoint of any country choosing which to align with, the choice presents a real trade-off. Reform from within, Risse adds, is "difficult ... though not impossible." The trilemma is therefore not a metaphysical impossibility; it is a description of the world as it stands.</p>

  <div class="context-box">
    <div class="context-box-label">Background — What is the Brussels Effect?</div>
    <p>The <strong>Brussels Effect</strong>, named by the legal scholar <strong>Anu Bradford</strong> in her 2020 book of the same title, describes the phenomenon by which European Union regulation comes to govern global commercial conduct without any equivalent rule existing elsewhere. The mechanism is straightforward: rather than maintain two product specifications, multinationals adopt the EU rule as the default and apply it to all markets. GDPR is the canonical case; competition law, food safety, and chemical regulation are others.</p>
    <p>Risse coins a deliberate counterpart he calls the <strong>Brussels paradox</strong>: the jurisdiction with the most thoughtful AI regulatory intentions has the least power over the technology it is trying to govern. The two terms are not contradictory. The Brussels Effect describes a mechanism that works best when companies have strong incentives to remain in the European market and when the regulated activities are visible. For frontier AI training, neither condition holds as cleanly.</p>
  </div>

  <p>The framing matters for how the rest of the argument lands. By setting human rights as the criterion against which the three regimes are measured, Risse puts a thumb on the scale that some readers will resist. His commentary is published by the Carr-Ryan Center for Human Rights, which is in the business of making this kind of argument, and Risse himself is the author of <em>Political Theory of the Digital Age</em> (2023), a book whose title signals exactly the framework being applied here. None of this disqualifies the diagnosis. It does mean the trilemma is not value-neutral. It is a moral claim using a tripartite typology as its instrument.</p>

  <span class="section-number">§ 02 — China</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Reach and capability, no <em>constraint</em></h2>
  <p class="section-standfirst">China has, in Risse's framing, governance reach and significant technological power. What it lacks — and what its regulatory architecture has been deliberately structured to lack — is rights commitment that operates as a real check on state authority. The legal instruments resemble those of rights-protective regimes; the subordination of those instruments to party authority is the structural fact.</p>

  <p>The body of digital law is extensive: the Cybersecurity Law (2017), the Data Security Law (2021), and the Personal Information Protection Law (2021), the last of which on paper bears resemblance to GDPR. But all of these instruments, Risse notes, are "explicitly subordinated to the requirements of 'national security' and 'social stability,'" which in operational terms means the requirements of party control. Data-protection norms apply horizontally between citizens and corporations; vertical state access to data is preserved without meaningful independent oversight. The result is a system that provides the appearance of rights-oriented governance while keeping the surveillance and control infrastructure intact.</p>

  <p>The consequences Risse traces are concrete. The social credit system, he notes, is frequently mischaracterized in Western media as a single nationally unified scoring mechanism; it is in reality a collection of local experiments, sectoral compliance systems, and national blacklists for specific violations, varying enormously in scope across localities. Acknowledging that variation, Risse argues, is "not merely a gesture toward nuance"; it is analytically important. But it does not change the structural diagnosis. Innovation zones in Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, and Guangdong are centrally authorized experiments, not independently governed spaces. None of them has institutional checks on party authority over digital systems. The direction of variation matters: localities compete to demonstrate technological leadership and alignment with central goals, not to provide stronger rights protections.</p>

  <p>Hong Kong is, in Risse's account, the limiting illustration. The 1997 handover came with a fifty-year promise that capitalist institutions and many freedoms would persist; the 2020 National Security Law, and the March 2024 expansion known as Article 23, have not kept it. The same legal framework used to silence the media tycoon Jimmy Lai — sentenced in late 2025 to twenty years in prison at the age of seventy-eight — also governs data access, platform regulation, and the subordination of algorithmic tools to party authority. The trajectory of the territory illustrates the same structural principle that runs through the rest of China's digital regulatory architecture: formal protections persist where they do not threaten party control, and are overridden when they do.</p>

  <p>Xinjiang is the limit case in another direction. Risse cites the August 2022 OHCHR assessment, which described what is happening in the region — Integrated Joint Operations Platforms, predictive policing, dense facial-recognition networks, DNA and voice-print databases, compulsory surveillance software on personal devices, applied to a Uyghur Muslim population of around twelve million — as potentially constituting crimes against humanity. The U.S. State Department has used the word genocide. Risse is careful: the role of AI here is not incidental but structural. AI is the infrastructure of surveillance and the mechanism by which risk scores generate detentions. And, as he notes, the model is being exported. As far back as 2019, the Carnegie Endowment documented Chinese AI surveillance technology in at least seventy-five countries. The exports carry hardware and software, but also governance templates designed to maximize state visibility over populations.</p>

  <p>Risse does not dismiss the case for the Chinese government's record on poverty alleviation, and he flags it explicitly: hundreds of millions lifted from poverty over decades is a real achievement, and the integration of AI throughout Chinese society is one expression of a public-good commitment that draws on both Communist and Confucian traditions. The point is that this is consistent with the diagnosis, not a reply to it. The structural lesson is that effective governance is not inherently good news from a rights standpoint. "Everything depends on what it governs <em>for</em>." Where reach and capability are high but rights commitment is absent or uneven, governance becomes "a force multiplier for repression rather than a constraint upon it."</p>

  <div class="section-divider">Part three</div>

  <span class="section-number">§ 03 — The United States</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Power without <em>governance</em></h2>
  <p class="section-standfirst">The United States houses, by the Stanford HAI Index's count, the greatest concentration of frontier AI development in the world: the majority of foundation-model labs, the dominant cloud infrastructure providers, the largest pools of capital directed at the field. What it has not built, Risse argues, is a regulatory architecture that translates this position into governance. The reasons are durable rather than mysterious.</p>

  <p>The political economy is the constraint. Technology firms have operated with extraordinary freedom from regulation; the ideology of innovation as inherently beneficial has been hegemonic across most of the political spectrum; and corporate lobbying has successfully blocked federal legislation on privacy, platform accountability, and data protection for over two decades. Digital technologies in the U.S., Risse argues, "have not been used to strengthen democratic governance" but to build something else.</p>

  <div class="context-box">
    <div class="context-box-label">Background — What is surveillance capitalism?</div>
    <p><strong>Surveillance capitalism</strong> is the term <strong>Shoshana Zuboff</strong> developed in her 2019 book <em>The Age of Surveillance Capitalism</em> for a specific and historically novel arrangement: the large-scale extraction of behavioral data from human experience, the construction of predictive models from that data, and the use of those models to modify behavior at scale, primarily for advertising and engagement revenue. The key move, in Zuboff's analysis, is not that data is collected — collection is old — but that human experience itself is converted into a tradeable commodity, with behavioral modification as the product.</p>
    <p>The framing is contested in its details, but it has become a reference point because it names what was happening gradually: a fundamental restructuring of the relationship between individuals and information systems, accomplished without democratic deliberation or consent. Zuboff currently co-directs the Carr-Ryan Center's "Surveillance Capitalism or Democracy?" Fellowship with Risse, which is part of the framing context.</p>
  </div>

  <p>The privacy gap is concrete. As of 2025, more than 140 countries have enacted national data-protection legislation; the United States is not among them. The vacuum has been filled at the state level — California's CCPA, strengthened by the CPRA, and equivalent legislation in Colorado, Connecticut, Virginia, and a growing list — but the result is fragmented, easier for large well-resourced companies to navigate than for smaller actors or individuals, and leaves large categories of data and processing outside its scope. The patchwork is meaningful; it is not adequate.</p>

  <p>The recent federal trajectory is, in Risse's view, one of active retreat. The 2022 <em>Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights</em> articulated non-binding principles. Executive Order 14110, issued in October 2023, went further: it required developers of powerful foundation models to share safety-test results with the federal government, directed agencies to assess civil-rights implications of AI in their areas of responsibility, and linked AI governance explicitly to privacy and consumer protection. Within days of taking office in January 2025, the Trump administration rescinded EO 14110, replaced it with EO 14179 — which reframed the federal role as enabling rather than governing AI development — and signaled a turn toward deregulation.</p>

  <p>Simultaneously, the institutional capacity that had been the vehicle for federal AI safety work has been dismantled. Roughly 500 probationary employees at the National Institute of Standards and Technology were targeted for layoff in February 2025, including most of the U.S. AI Safety Institute (AISI). NIST then issued a directive instructing partner researchers to remove references to "AI safety," "responsible AI," and "AI fairness" from their cooperative agreement objectives. AISI's director, Elizabeth Kelly, departed before the layoffs were finalized. AISI staff were excluded from the Paris AI Action Summit in February 2025, where Vice President JD Vance argued that "excessive regulation" would "kill a transformative industry just as it's taking off." The U.S. and U.K. did not sign the summit's final declaration.</p>

  <div class="inline-note">
    <strong>The institutional gap.</strong> AISI was created in November 2023 as the Biden administration's primary instrument for federal AI safety research — model evaluations, red-teaming, voluntary frameworks with frontier labs. Its successor function, after the February 2025 disruption, has not been established at the federal level. The state-level patchwork remains; the federal coordinating capacity that NIST had begun to build does not.
  </div>

  <p>The remaining federal architecture — HIPAA in health, the Fair Credit Reporting Act in finance, COPPA for children's data, civil-rights statutes applied by FTC and EEOC enforcement — is not nothing, but is structurally inadequate in a way that goes beyond mere incompleteness. The sectoral approach was designed for a world of discrete industries handling discrete categories of data. AI, Risse argues, is not a sector; it is a general-purpose technology whose most consequential effects arise from precisely the cross-domain aggregation that sector-specific laws were designed to prevent. A regulatory architecture built on sector boundaries cannot govern a technology that dissolves them.</p>

  <p>The structural lesson is the inverse of China's. Where technological power is enormous but governance is weak and fragmented, the consequence is not state repression but market-driven erosion of privacy, autonomy, and equality — distributed, less visible, cumulatively serious. Kate Crawford's <em>Atlas of AI</em> and Virginia Eubanks's <em>Automating Inequality</em> have documented what this looks like at the level of hiring, benefits, criminal justice, and content moderation: outcomes that fall hardest on those with the least power, with decision-makers insulated from accountability. Risse adds, characteristically, that he has written elsewhere about the United States' deficits in rights commitment as well; the claim of the trilemma is not that each bloc has two of the three, but that each has <em>at most</em> two. The distinction is meant to set a ceiling, not a floor.</p>

  <span class="section-number">§ 04 — The European Union</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Rights without <em>industrial leverage</em></h2>
  <p class="section-standfirst">The EU is the most serious of the three actors in its commitment to making AI governance answer to human rights, Risse argues, but it exercises limited control over the technology itself. The instruments are sophisticated. The reach extends only as far as the market does, and the most consequential parts of frontier AI are built outside the bloc.</p>

  <p>The legal architecture is the most developed of the three. The AI Act, formally adopted in 2024 as Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, takes a risk-tiered approach: outright prohibition of "unacceptable risk" applications — social scoring, manipulation of psychological vulnerabilities, most real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, predictive policing based solely on profiling — and strict requirements around transparency, human oversight, data quality, and fundamental-rights impact assessments for systems classified as "high-risk" in employment, education, law enforcement, migration, and access to essential services. Layered with GDPR (in force since 2018, treating data protection as a fundamental right under the Charter) and the Digital Services Act (which requires large platforms to conduct systemic risk assessments and provide researchers with data access), the EU framework is, on paper, the most coherent attempt to make rights commitments operational in digital governance.</p>

  <p>Risse is honest about the AI Act's weaknesses. It was significantly shaped by concerns about narrower, more predictable AI systems and was somewhat overtaken by generative AI before it came into force; its treatment of general-purpose models was added late and remains less developed than the rest. Implementation carries real friction. National enforcement authorities may not have the technical expertise to supervise sophisticated systems. And civil-society critique — particularly from European Digital Rights — has pointed out that the Act, despite its prohibitions, still permits significant uses of AI in law enforcement and migration control under broadly drawn national-security exceptions, with the highest-stakes rights situations sitting precisely where the framework is least firm.</p>

  <p>The structural fact, however, is not the Act's internal compromises. It is the geography of the technology it governs. Frontier foundation models — the systems now reshaping how information is processed, content generated, decisions made — are predominantly built by U.S.-headquartered companies (OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta) or Chinese ones (Baidu, Alibaba, Huawei). The compute infrastructure, the data centers and specialized chips that make large-scale training possible, is concentrated outside Europe. Risse names this "the <strong>Brussels paradox</strong>": the jurisdiction with the most thoughtful regulatory intentions has the least power over the technology it is trying to govern.</p>

  <p>The Brussels Effect — extraterritorial regulation through market access — partially compensates. Companies adjust products and practices to comply with European law rather than be excluded from a 450-million-person single market. But this mechanism, Risse argues, works best where companies have strong incentives to remain in the European market and where the regulated activities are visible. For frontier AI training — the architectural choices, the dataset selection, the safety evaluations — neither condition holds cleanly, and European leverage is "partial at best."</p>

  <p>Two developments since Risse wrote sharpen the EU picture. The first concerns the Digital Omnibus, the European Commission's reform package proposed on 19 November 2025. Its centerpiece was a sixteen-month postponement of the AI Act's high-risk obligations under Annex III — from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027 — to give standards bodies and national competent authorities time to be ready. The trilogue stalled on 28 April, in the breakdown that N° 03 documented. On 7 May 2026 — two days ago — the Council and Parliament reached provisional agreement, broadly preserving the postponements while clarifying interplay with sectoral regimes. The Omnibus has yet to be formally adopted, but the political settlement is now in hand. The fight that broke down at two in the morning has resolved.</p>

  <p>Whether this strengthens or weakens the EU's claim depends on what one thinks the August deadline was supposed to achieve. The Commission's case was that the original timeline required compliance against standards that did not yet exist — a deadline that would have produced ambiguity rather than rigor. The civil-society objection is that postponement is the camel's nose: each extension widens the space in which uncovered systems are deployed, and a framework that bends to deadline pressure is harder to defend the next time. Both readings are within Risse's frame. The EU still has rights commitment without industrial leverage; the only question is whether the deferral was an act of competent administration or one of deference to the bloc's missing leg.</p>

  <p>The second development is the Trump administration's December 2025 executive order — and the subsequent Department of Justice intervention against Colorado's algorithmic-discrimination law on Equal Protection grounds — which N° 04 took up at length. Colorado SB24-205 was, architecturally, a near-translation of the EU AI Act. The federal action against it is, in the trilemma's terms, the United States not merely failing to govern AI but actively closing off the only level — the states — at which European-style governance had begun to migrate into American law. The Brussels Effect, in other words, has a counter-current. Whether it holds is now a constitutional question.</p>

  <div class="section-divider">Part five</div>

  <span class="section-number">§ 05 — The reform agenda and the window</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Each bloc has a <em>concrete</em> agenda, if it chose</h2>
  <p class="section-standfirst">The trilemma framework is generative as well as diagnostic. Each missing condition implies a reform that, by Risse's lights, is already specifiable. The harder claim is the temporal one: the conditions for any of these reforms are narrower today than they were five years ago, and they will be narrower five years from now.</p>

  <p>The bloc-specific prescriptions are direct. China's regulatory sophistication in the absence of rights commitment "calls for international pressure on transparency and on the conditions attached to AI exports" — particularly to the Global South countries that import its surveillance infrastructure. The United States' technological power in the absence of governance "calls for federal privacy legislation, strengthened agency enforcement, state-level harmonization, and the restoration and strengthening of the Biden-era frameworks that the current administration has abandoned." The EU's rights commitment without technological power "calls for serious investment in European AI capacity — compute infrastructure, foundation-model development, data ecosystems — without which the most sophisticated regulatory framework in the world governs only the margins of the technology it is meant to steer."</p>

  <p>Risse extends the case beyond bloc-specific reform. A global moratorium on frontier AI development would be the most serious possible expression of collective responsibility for what is being built; he says explicitly that he continues to believe it would be sensible. He also concedes that arguing for a moratorium without a theory of how it could be achieved is "not a prescription; it is a wish." What is more immediately achievable, in his view, is a more specific set of international commitments: mandatory incident reporting for significant AI failures, modeled on aviation safety reporting; agreement on a small set of categorical prohibitions, including AI-enabled mass surveillance for export and fully autonomous lethal-weapons decisions; and a dedicated international fund for AI governance capacity in lower-income countries, which face the highest risks with the least institutional protection.</p>

  <p>The case for international coordination has, Risse acknowledges, dimmed since January 2025. The Bletchley Declaration of November 2023 — signed by the United States, the United Kingdom, China, the EU, and twenty-five other parties — committed governments to cooperative safety research and acknowledged that AI risks "are inherently international in nature." Bletchley was followed by Seoul in 2024 (a narrower communiqué, eleven signatories) and the Paris AI Action Summit of February 2025, where sixty-one countries — including China — signed a declaration on inclusive and sustainable AI, and the United States and the United Kingdom did not. Vance's speech at Paris reframed the project: not safety, not rights, but innovation; the U.K. recast its own AI Safety Institute as the AI Security Institute days later, dropping bias and disinformation work explicitly. As Risse summarizes the arc: roughly a decade of advocacy for integrating ethics and human rights into AI governance had followed a period of "deliberately curated apathy"; the Trump administration has now ushered in a period of "active abdication."</p>

  <p>The window argument deserves to be taken seriously, in Risse's framing. Nuclear non-proliferation governance was possible in the 1960s partly because it was negotiated before capability had spread irreversibly; that window is now much narrower. Platform regulation became dramatically harder once social-media companies had grown into infrastructure — the Digital Services Act is an attempt to close a window already partly closed. The cost of delaying climate governance is now measurable in degrees of warming. AI faces the same dynamic: the more deeply AI systems embed themselves into economic infrastructure, state functions, and the daily texture of social life, the higher the political and technical cost of subjecting them to meaningful accountability.</p>

  <div class="pull-quote">
    What is missing is not the tools but the will to use them.
    <span class="pull-quote-attribution">— Mathias Risse</span>
  </div>

  <p>Risse's closing claim is therefore not pessimistic in the analytical sense. The trilemma is "not a logical impossibility — it is a political condition, and political conditions can change." Each bloc has a clear agenda if it chooses to act on it. The international community has institutions, however imperfect, that could be strengthened and redirected. The normative framework — human rights as a genuine constraint on both state and market power — is "available, codified in international law, and ratified by all three jurisdictions under discussion." The phrasing is precise: ratified, in the case of all three. What is missing is not the tools but the will to use them.</p>

  <div class="section-divider">Coda</div>

  <p>What is genuinely uncertain. Whether the trilemma is becoming sharper or softer. The EU has just preserved the bulk of its postponement-and-streamlining package; if formally adopted, the Omnibus relieves implementation pressure but reopens questions civil society had taken as settled. The United States is not failing to govern AI by accident; it is, on the current trajectory, deliberately closing off the institutional capacity that had begun to do so, including at the state level via federal litigation. China continues to export both surveillance technology and the templates that make state visibility over populations the operating premise. Three trajectories, none aligned.</p>

  <p>What is not uncertain. The diagnostic frame is useful regardless of which way each regime turns next. Risse's contribution is less the trilemma label — typologies of AI governance proliferate — than the insistence that the third leg, rights commitment, is what makes the whole question a moral one rather than a procurement one. A regime can have governance and capability and still produce a society no rights-respecting reader would want to live in. A regime can have rights and capability and still fail to govern what it builds. A regime can have rights and governance and still be a footnote to a technology built elsewhere. The trilemma is a way of refusing to count any one of these as a victory.</p>

  <p>The piece reads, in the end, as a philosopher's argument and not a policy paper. It does not score reform proposals against feasibility constraints; it does not weigh the relative costs of different international architectures; it does not engage at length with the work of those who reject the rights-as-criterion frame in favor of, say, capability or competition or sovereignty. What it does is hold the question on its own ground. That ground is moral, the criterion is human rights, and the diagnosis is that nobody in charge of AI today is meeting all three of the conditions human rights requires. The reader is left with a picture in which the missing leg in each case is exactly the leg the regime is least likely to build on its own — and a window the author insists is still open, but only just.</p>
