<Lede>The article opens with what was, in 2012, a heterodox claim. Europe in the years after the eurozone crisis was widely described as a power in decline: militarily weak, demographically stagnant, monetarily fragile, geopolitically irrelevant, condemned by its inability to speak with one voice to a kind of soft-multilateral irrelevance. Anu Bradford — then, as now, a professor of law at Columbia, formerly of the University of Chicago, with prior training at the University of Helsinki and a clerkship background in EU institutional law — argued that this consensus had missed something essential.<Footnote n={1}/> Outside the conventional registers of power, the European Union had built an extraordinarily effective mechanism for projecting its preferences onto the rest of the world. It did so without armies, without sanctions, without the cooperation of other states, and largely without the consent of the populations whose daily lives it was shaping. It did so through the unilateral promulgation of regulations that the global market had reasons of its own to obey. The phenomenon already had a name in domestic American legal scholarship — the California Effect, theorised by David Vogel in 1995 — but it had not been described systematically as a feature of international regulatory order. Bradford's contribution was to provide that systematic account, to identify the precise conditions under which the mechanism operates, to trace its empirical manifestations across five regulatory domains, and to argue that the EU, in the early twenty-first century, was the only jurisdiction in which all of the necessary conditions converged. The sixty-seven pages that followed have, over the intervening fifteen years, become canonical: the analytical template against which subsequent scholarship on EU regulatory power, and most of the literature on the global governance of digital systems, has been written.</Lede>

<Section number="01" title="The opening provocation, and what unilateral regulatory globalisation <em>means</em>">

<Standfirst>The conceptual core of Bradford's argument is a distinction. Most scholarship on international regulatory convergence treats convergence as the product of either cooperation or coercion. Bradford identifies a third pathway that operates between the two — and which, she argues, accounts for a substantial and underappreciated share of contemporary international regulatory order.</Standfirst>

The opening rhetorical move is calibrated. Bradford begins not with the framework but with a catalogue of unfamiliar facts about familiar things. EU regulations, she observes, determine the cosmetics Americans apply in the morning, the cereal they eat for breakfast, the software architecture they use on their computers, the privacy settings governing their Facebook accounts, the interoffice telephone directories they use at work, the air conditioners that cool their homes, and the absence of soft plastic toys in their children's McDonald's Happy Meals.<Footnote n={2}/> The catalogue is not incidental. It establishes that the phenomenon being theorised is not abstract: it operates pervasively, in domains so close to everyday life that the absence of any American political consent to the rules governing them registers as a kind of quiet scandal once it is pointed out.

From this catalogue, Bradford moves to the conceptual distinction that defines the rest of the article. Political regulatory globalisation occurs when states converge on common standards through negotiated agreements: treaties, conventions, regulatory dialogues, harmonisation regimes administered by international institutions such as the WTO or the OECD. Coercive regulatory globalisation occurs when one state imposes its standards on another through threats, sanctions, or conditional incentives — the canonical example being the United States' use of economic sanctions to compel adoption of its anti-terrorism and anti-narcotics regimes. Unilateral regulatory globalisation, the phenomenon Bradford names the Brussels Effect, is neither.<Footnote n={3}/> It is the migration of one jurisdiction's standards into another's territory without the former actively imposing them or the latter willingly adopting them. The mechanism is the market itself.

<ContextBox label="Anu Bradford's broader project, in context">
Bradford is a Finnish-American legal scholar at Columbia Law School whose work has reframed the literature on EU regulatory power. The 2012 article being read here gave the concept its name; her 2020 monograph, *The Brussels Effect: How the European Union Rules the World* (Oxford University Press), extended the framework into a book-length empirical demonstration across data protection, antitrust, environmental policy, food safety, hate speech, and consumer health. Her 2023 *Digital Empires* situates the Brussels Effect within a broader comparative analysis of three competing models of digital governance — American market-driven, Chinese state-driven, and European rights-driven. The 2012 article remains the theoretical foundation on which the later books rest; its arguments are not superseded by the later work but elaborated, supplemented with newer cases, and stress-tested against critics. For the doctoral researcher, the 2012 article is the primary text; the 2020 book is the extended empirical defence; and *Digital Empires* is the framing within which Bradford situates her own theory among rivals.
</ContextBox>

The intellectual antecedent that Bradford acknowledges most explicitly is David Vogel's *Trading Up*, the 1995 book that theorised the California Effect.<Footnote n={4}/> Vogel observed that California, by virtue of its market size and preference for strict consumer and environmental standards, was able to set de facto national standards within the United States. Manufacturers seeking access to the California market complied with California rules; the scale economies of uniform production then propagated those rules to their entire output. Bradford's move was to recognise that the same logic operates at a global scale, that the EU plays California's role in the international system, and that the existing literature on regulatory globalisation had failed to account for this. The framework she develops is intended to identify the precise conditions that enable a jurisdiction to exercise this kind of unilateral influence, the mechanism by which it propagates, and the structural limits that constrain it.

Importantly, Bradford situates her argument against three rival theoretical traditions. The first is the "race to the bottom" literature, which holds that international economic integration leads to a downward spiral in regulatory standards as jurisdictions compete to attract capital. The second is the literature on great-power regulatory politics, exemplified by Daniel Drezner's work, which holds that international regulatory convergence depends on the consensus or disagreement between dominant powers.<Footnote n={5}/> The third is the literature on cooperative regulatory harmonisation, exemplified by Beth Simmons' work on capital adequacy standards, which models convergence as the voluntary adoption by lenient regulators of stricter rules in pursuit of capital flows. Bradford's contribution sits in the gap between these accounts. The Brussels Effect is not a race to the bottom: it produces convergence on stricter, not laxer, standards. It is not driven by great-power consensus: it operates against the explicit disagreement of the United States. And it is not cooperative: foreign corporations would prefer to follow another rule and adjust only because the opportunity cost of forgoing the EU market is too high.

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="The first three conditions: market power, capacity, and the <em>preference</em> for stringency">

<Standfirst>The article advances a five-part theoretical framework. A jurisdiction can globalise its standards unilaterally if, and only if, it satisfies all five conditions. The framework is the analytical heart of the paper, and Bradford insists on its cumulative structure: each condition is individually necessary, and the conditions are jointly sufficient.</Standfirst>

The first condition — market power — is the precondition most discussed in prior literature, and the one Bradford treats with the least theoretical originality. A jurisdiction must have a large enough internal market that exclusion from it imposes opportunity costs sufficient to induce foreign producers to absorb the adjustment costs of compliance. Bradford operationalises this with care. What matters is not simply market size in absolute terms, but the ratio of exports to the strict jurisdiction relative to the exporter's home-market sales and alternative-market opportunities. The greater that ratio, the more sensitive the exporter is to the loss of EU access, and the more willing it will be to comply with EU rules even when they substantially exceed home-market requirements. The EU's roughly 500 million consumers, its $16 trillion GDP at the time of writing (purchasing-power-parity adjusted), and its position as the world's largest importer of goods and services supplied this condition with substantial margin.<Footnote n={6}/> Bradford notes that the United States, China, and Japan also satisfy the market-size threshold considered alone — but, as the subsequent conditions reveal, they do not satisfy the full set.

The second condition — regulatory capacity — is more analytically distinctive. Market power alone is inert without institutions capable of producing rules, enforcing them against domestic and foreign actors, and imposing sanctions for non-compliance. Bradford argues that this condition disqualifies most large markets. China, despite its consumer base of 1.3 billion, lacked at the time of writing both the regulatory institutions and the enforcement track record to project rules abroad; its antitrust regime, for instance, dated only to 2008 and was distributed across three separate enforcement agencies whose mutual coordination was uncertain. India, Brazil, and the other large emerging economies were in similar positions. Among large markets, Bradford concludes, only the United States and the European Union genuinely satisfied this condition at the time of writing. The EU's regulatory capacity, she emphasises, is the product of a particular institutional history: the European Commission's central role in market-creation, the gradual expansion of qualified majority voting that reduced the unanimity bottleneck, the strengthening of the European Parliament as a co-legislator with pro-regulation tendencies, and the European Court of Justice's willingness to back regulatory expansion with binding interpretation.<Footnote n={7}/> Critically, regulatory capacity is unevenly distributed across policy domains: it is greatest in trade, competition, and single-market domains where member states have ceded exclusive or shared competence to the EU, and least in domains such as common foreign and security policy where the member states have retained control.

<InlineNote>The capacity condition is more than an institutional checklist. It implicitly carries an enforcement-credibility requirement: foreign actors must believe that non-compliance will be detected and sanctioned at sufficient cost to make compliance the rational strategy.</InlineNote>

The third condition is the political precondition: a domestic preference for strict regulation. In contests between a lax regulator and a strict one over the same conduct, the strict one prevails by definition — its rule binds the firm operating in both markets. Bradford traces the historical reversal that placed the EU rather than the United States in this role. Until the 1980s, U.S. consumer and environmental standards were the strictest in the OECD; American multinationals were the global standard-setters, and European firms adjusted to their requirements. By the 2000s, that position had migrated decisively to Brussels.<Footnote n={8}/> Bradford identifies a set of proximate causes for the reversal. European publics, surveyed across multiple Eurobarometer waves, consistently rank environmental and food-safety concerns higher than crime or terrorism — a risk-perception inversion of the post-9/11 American pattern. EU constitutional identity, articulated explicitly in the Lisbon Treaty's commitment to a "highly competitive social market economy," builds regulatory stringency into the political-economic settlement of the union itself.<Footnote n={9}/> European political elites are less ideologically polarised than their American counterparts on questions of regulatory intervention, and consequently more responsive to public demands for new rules. And the EU's adoption of the precautionary principle as a binding rule of regulatory decision-making, articulated in the Commission's 2000 communication and embedded in subsequent jurisprudence, lowers the evidentiary threshold for intervention compared to the U.S. cost-benefit standard.<Footnote n={10}/> The cumulative effect is a regulatory political economy systematically tilted toward stringency — a tilt that, once established, becomes self-reinforcing as export-oriented European firms lobby for the global propagation of standards they have already absorbed.

Bradford is careful to note that this condition does not require Europe to be more rigorous on every issue. The United States retains stricter rules in certain domains — tobacco regulation, some areas of financial regulation, and various sector-specific areas where American risk perceptions differ. The relevant question is which jurisdiction is stricter on the specific issue at stake. Where the EU is stricter, and where the other conditions hold, the EU rule prevails. Where the U.S. is stricter, the same logic applies in reverse, though Bradford argues this set of cases is now substantially smaller than the EU set.

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="Inelastic targets, and the three forms of <em>nondivisibility</em>">

<Standfirst>The fourth and fifth conditions are the structural ones — the conditions that explain not whether the EU could in principle project regulatory power abroad but in which specific domains it actually does so, and through what mechanism.</Standfirst>

The fourth condition — predisposition to regulate inelastic targets — narrows the regulatory space in which the Brussels Effect can operate. Strict standards globalise only if they regulate targets that cannot escape by moving. Consumers cannot relocate to circumvent product safety rules; capital can. The EU's focus on consumer markets, food safety, chemical regulation, and competition policy reflects this structural constraint — and explains why labour regulation, financial transactions, and corporate taxation are areas where the Effect operates weakly or not at all. Bradford develops this distinction carefully, acknowledging that elasticity is not a binary property but a continuum. Capital is more elastic than consumers but less elastic than, say, stock exchange listings. Stock exchange listings are more elastic than corporate tax domiciles, which are in turn more elastic than physical manufacturing facilities. Each regulatory domain inhabits a different position on this elasticity continuum, and the Brussels Effect's operative scope corresponds to the inelastic end.<Footnote n={11}/>

The choice of regulatory targets is, in this account, partly endogenous to the EU's strategic position. By concentrating its regulatory ambitions on consumer markets and other inelastic domains, the EU systematically deploys its regulatory capacity in precisely the areas where its rules cannot be circumvented through market-driven exit. This is not necessarily the product of conscious strategic design — Bradford is explicit that the EU's external regulatory power is largely an inadvertent by-product of internal market-building — but it has the strategic consequence of insulating the EU's chosen regulatory targets from the race-to-the-bottom pressures that would otherwise erode its standards.

The fifth condition — nondivisibility — is the mechanism through which a regulation that formally applies only inside the EU becomes a regulation that effectively applies everywhere. This is the article's most important analytical contribution, and Bradford develops it with substantial care. She distinguishes three forms.

**Legal nondivisibility** occurs when the regulated transaction cannot be partitioned across jurisdictions as a matter of law or institutional fact. The canonical example is a global merger: a transaction that creates a new combined entity cannot proceed in one market and be enjoined in another, because there is only one transaction to consummate. The strictest competition regulator's veto therefore becomes the global outcome, regardless of how many other jurisdictions have cleared the deal. Similar logic applies to other domains where the regulated object is intrinsically unitary: a global corporate structure, a global brand identity, a binding global standard for an international financial instrument.

**Technical nondivisibility** occurs when the architecture of the regulated activity does not permit jurisdictional segregation as a matter of engineering or technical implementation. Data flows are the paradigm case. A global search engine cannot easily build separate technical infrastructures for European and non-European users; the cookies, the server logs, the recommendation algorithms, the encryption protocols, the deletion procedures, and the data retention schedules are constructed once and deployed everywhere. The cost of building parallel technical stacks is, for most providers, prohibitive — both in capital expenditure and in the ongoing operational complexity of maintaining two versions of the same product. Bradford observes this dynamic in detail in the context of the 1995 Data Protection Directive, and the dynamic has only intensified since with cloud computing, machine-learning training pipelines, and the technical interdependencies that bind modern software systems.

**Economic nondivisibility** occurs when scale economies make dual production uneconomical even when it is technically and legally feasible. A chemical manufacturer that has reformulated its products to meet REACH standards typically finds it cheaper to sell the reformulated product worldwide than to maintain two production processes, two supply chains, two regulatory documentation systems, and two distribution networks. The fixed costs of producing under REACH are sunk; the marginal cost of extending REACH-compliant production to non-EU markets is small; the alternative — running a second production line for non-EU markets — preserves only the variable cost differential and imposes substantial new fixed costs. For most manufacturers in most product categories, the calculation favours global compliance.

<PullQuote attribution="— Anu Bradford">Market forces are sufficient to create involuntary incentives to adjust to the rules of the strict regulator. In other words, unilateral regulatory globalization entails the dominant jurisdiction imposing an incentive to adjust, followed by reluctant emulation by market participants.</PullQuote>

The three forms of nondivisibility are not exhaustive but they are mutually reinforcing. A single regulatory domain may exhibit all three: a global merger (legal nondivisibility) may involve global data systems (technical nondivisibility) and global manufacturing operations (economic nondivisibility). In such cases the Brussels Effect operates with particular intensity. Conversely, regulatory domains in which divisibility prevails — labour standards, much of consumer protection at the service level, certain narrow categories of financial regulation — are the domains in which the Effect is weakest.

</Section>

<Section number="04" title="The cascade in two registers, <em>de facto</em> and <em>de jure</em>">

<Standfirst>The Brussels Effect operates in two registers, and the article is careful to distinguish them. The distinction has consequences for how empirical evidence of the phenomenon should be read, and for what kinds of resistance to the Effect are possible.</Standfirst>

The de facto Brussels Effect is the primary mechanism. It does not require any formal action by another state. A foreign firm reformulates its products, amends its data architecture, restructures its merger documentation, or alters its supply chain in order to retain access to the EU market. Once that adjustment has been made, the firm — for reasons of scale economy, technical architecture, or legal nondivisibility — applies the new standard to its entire global operation. The EU rule has become the global rule. No legislator outside the EU has formally adopted it. No treaty has been signed. No coercion has occurred. The market has done the work.<Footnote n={12}/>

The de jure Brussels Effect is the secondary mechanism. It emerges when firms that have already adjusted to EU standards lobby their home governments to adopt those same standards. The motive is partly defensive: the export-oriented firms that have absorbed the adjustment costs find themselves at a competitive disadvantage relative to domestic competitors who have not had to comply, and seek to level the playing field by extending EU rules to the whole domestic market. The motive is partly normative: civil society organisations and consumer advocates, observing that a stricter regulatory regime is now operationally feasible because their domestic firms are already complying with it, press for its formal incorporation into national law. The motive is partly bureaucratic: domestic regulators, who tend to view harmonised rules as administratively simpler, often welcome the convergence.

The two effects are sequential. De facto adjustment paves the way for de jure adoption — not the reverse. This inverts the implementation problem familiar to international legal scholarship: when a treaty requires firms to change their conduct, compliance is uncertain because the firms have not pre-committed; in the Brussels Effect sequence, formal legal adoption merely codifies a market reality already established. From the perspective of comparative regulatory politics, this sequencing has substantial consequences. Domestic legislatures considering whether to adopt EU-style rules are not asked to impose new costs on their firms; they are asked to ratify costs the firms have already absorbed. The political economy of adoption is correspondingly more favourable than it would be under the standard treaty model.

The article is emphatic that the de jure effect is not necessary for the Brussels Effect to operate. The Effect is essentially about one jurisdiction's capacity to override others — not about its capacity to trigger a formal race to the top. Bradford observes that the literature on the California Effect has tended to focus on the rarer and more visible de jure dimension, and in doing so has underestimated the breadth of the underlying phenomenon. Global commerce, in many domains, already operates under unilateral EU rules even where other jurisdictions formally maintain their own laws. The de jure effect, when it occurs, is best understood as the consolidation of an underlying market reality rather than as the primary mechanism of convergence.

This analytical clarification matters for empirical research. A researcher who looks only for formal legal convergence will systematically underestimate the Effect's scope, missing the cases where U.S. firms comply globally with EU rules while U.S. law remains formally divergent. The empirical literature on EU regulatory influence, Bradford suggests, must look at the operational practices of multinational firms rather than at the statute books of foreign jurisdictions. The Effect is, in her formulation, more about one jurisdiction's ability to override others than it is about triggering an upward race.

</Section>

<Section number="05" title="Case I — antitrust, and the <em>nondivisibility</em> of global mergers">

<Standfirst>The first of the article's case studies is built around legal nondivisibility. It centres on the Commission's prohibition of the General Electric–Honeywell merger and the broader pattern of EU competition enforcement against U.S. firms.</Standfirst>

The General Electric–Honeywell case, decided by the Commission on 3 July 2001, is the article's most extensively developed example.<Footnote n={13}/> The transaction was a forty-two-billion-dollar acquisition of Honeywell International by General Electric — two American companies, with their principal operations in the United States, and with their primary regulatory home in the U.S. Department of Justice's Antitrust Division. The DOJ had cleared the merger subject to relatively minor divestiture conditions. The Commission, applying its own merger control regulation and its own substantive theory of competitive effects, blocked it outright.

Bradford's interest in the case is not the merits but the structure. Two regulators applied two different substantive theories of competitive harm to the same transaction. The DOJ saw efficiency gains; the Commission saw the long-run elimination of competition through bundling and portfolio effects. There was no neutral arbiter, no international tribunal, no treaty obligation requiring either to defer to the other. And yet, because a merger is by its nature a single global transaction — one cannot consummate it in some jurisdictions and not others — the stricter regulator's veto determined the outcome worldwide. The DOJ's clearance was rendered operationally meaningless. General Electric and Honeywell, two American firms operating principally in American markets and cleared by American regulators, could not merge because Brussels said no.

The GE–Honeywell case is not isolated. Bradford documents a sustained pattern. The 1997 Boeing–McDonnell Douglas case followed the same logic in attenuated form: the Commission did not block the merger but extracted concessions, including the abandonment of Boeing's exclusive dealing contracts with U.S. carriers, that the FTC had specifically declined to require. The 1991 Aerospatiale–Alenia/de Havilland case showed the same dynamic earlier, with the Commission blocking a transaction approved by Canadian authorities. More recent cases — Bradford writes from the perspective of 2012 — include the EU's intervention against Microsoft's bundling practices (with record-high fines and global behavioural remedies), the proceedings against Intel for abuse of dominance, the De Beers case requiring divestiture of supply contracts with Russian producers, and the Intel/McAfee merger that required the unbundling of software and security solutions worldwide as a condition of clearance.<Footnote n={14}/>

The pattern, Bradford argues, is not driven by an asymmetry of regulatory capacity. Both the FTC and the DOJ have extraterritorial jurisdiction under the effects doctrine articulated in *Aluminum Co. of America* and codified in the Foreign Trade Antitrust Improvements Act; both have repeatedly applied U.S. antitrust law to foreign conduct affecting American markets. The asymmetry is one of regulatory preference. The Commission is simply more willing to intervene, more sceptical of efficiency justifications, more concerned about "false negatives" (anti-competitive conduct that goes unsanctioned) and less concerned about "false positives" (efficient conduct that is wrongly restricted) than its American counterparts. The Commission's confidence in its own ability to identify market failures and its scepticism of market self-correction reflect what Bradford characterises as a more statist tradition in European regulatory thought.

In one institutional respect the Commission's capacity actually exceeds the FTC's: it can prohibit mergers and impose behavioural and structural remedies through administrative decision without seeking judicial endorsement. The FTC, by contrast, must persuade a federal court to enjoin a merger. The European administrative state is, in this narrow but consequential respect, more powerful than the American one. Combined with the substantive preference for intervention and with the legal nondivisibility of merger transactions, this capacity makes the Commission the de facto global antitrust regulator in cases of any meaningful trans-Atlantic dimension.

Critics of the Commission, Bradford notes, have argued for a mutual-recognition regime in which each agency would defer to the other's decisions in matters primarily affecting the other's market. She regards this proposal as politically implausible. The Commission has no incentive to surrender authority over conduct affecting European markets, and any retreat would be seen domestically as a derogation of its core mandate. The U.S. authorities, for their part, have publicly conceded the Commission's right to apply its own laws to its own market. The asymmetry is stable.

</Section>

<Section number="06" title="Case II — privacy, technical <em>nondivisibility</em>, and the canonical example">

<Standfirst>The second case study is built around technical nondivisibility and addresses what has become, in the years since the article appeared, the most canonical empirical demonstration of the Brussels Effect.</Standfirst>

The privacy case is in some ways the article's centrepiece — not because Bradford develops it at greater length than the others but because the dynamic it describes has, in the intervening decade and a half, become the paradigmatic illustration of the Effect. Bradford's analysis is grounded in the 1995 Data Protection Directive, the legal instrument that established the EU's distinctive approach to personal-data regulation.<Footnote n={15}/> The Directive treated privacy as a fundamental right rather than a contractual interest, applied comprehensively to both public and private sectors, required member states to establish independent data protection authorities, and — most consequentially for the externalisation dynamic — prohibited the transfer of personal data to third countries that did not provide "adequate" protection, with adequacy assessed by the Commission on a case-by-case basis.

The U.S. approach is structurally different. American privacy law operates sectorally rather than comprehensively, with the strongest protections concentrated in particular domains (health information under HIPAA, financial information under Gramm-Leach-Bliley, children's data under COPPA) and the bulk of private-sector activity governed by industry self-regulation supervised by the Federal Trade Commission's broad authority over unfair or deceptive acts and practices. There is no federal omnibus data protection statute and no independent privacy authority of the European type. Consumers, in the American model, are expected to contract with firms for the level of privacy they want; the law sets a floor but treats privacy primarily as an alienable consumer preference.

The structural divergence created the conditions for technical nondivisibility to operate. A global firm cannot easily maintain different privacy architectures for its European and non-European users. The data architectures that store, process, and transmit user information are unitary by design; the algorithms that recommend content, target advertising, and personalise services rely on training data drawn globally; the engineering teams that build and maintain these systems do so once and deploy everywhere. To comply with the Directive's restrictions on data transfer to inadequate jurisdictions, a firm must either avoid transferring European data to its U.S. servers (creating substantial operational complexity and forcing a re-architecture of cloud services) or, more commonly, raise its global privacy practices to EU-compatible levels. The latter is, for most firms, the cheaper option.

Bradford documents this dynamic across a series of firms by 2012. General Motors, DuPont, and Procter & Gamble had applied EU-style standards to employee data worldwide. Microsoft had reached an agreement with EU authorities to implement company-wide data protection policies for its services. Google had altered its data retention and street-view collection practices globally in response to EU pressure. The Safe Harbor framework, negotiated between the Commerce Department and the Commission in 2000, formalised a route by which U.S. firms could self-certify compliance with EU-equivalent protections and thereby qualify for cross-Atlantic data transfers; thousands of firms eventually enrolled. By Bradford's count, over thirty countries had by 2012 adopted EU-style privacy legislation, including most OECD members other than the United States.<Footnote n={16}/>

The intervening years have validated and extended this analysis with what is now considered the textbook empirical case for the Brussels Effect: the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which came into force in May 2018 and replaced the 1995 Directive with a substantially more demanding and extraterritorially explicit regulation. The GDPR applies extraterritorially to any firm processing the personal data of EU residents regardless of where the firm is established; it imposes fines of up to four per cent of global annual revenue; and it has been adopted, in substance if not always in form, by jurisdictions ranging from Brazil (LGPD, 2018) and California (CCPA, 2018; CPRA, 2020) to India (DPDP Act, 2023) and a long list of others. The post-2012 empirical record, in other words, has been more favourable to Bradford's framework than even the article's most assertive predictions would have anticipated.

The relevance for current AI-governance debates is direct. The GDPR's evolution has not occurred in isolation; it has interlocked with the AI Act and with the broader Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act regulatory complex. The technical nondivisibility that propagated the Directive's privacy norms is the same technical nondivisibility that will, in many cases, propagate the AI Act's obligations on documentation, transparency, risk assessment, and human oversight to AI systems deployed globally. The chain of reasoning that runs from Bradford 2012 through GDPR to AI Act compliance is, for the doctoral researcher working on regulatory interoperability, the central empirical-theoretical sequence.

</Section>

<Section number="07" title="Cases III and IV — REACH, RoHS, and the chemistry of <em>economic</em> nondivisibility">

<Standfirst>The third and fourth case studies turn from technical to economic nondivisibility — the situations in which scale economies, rather than the architecture of the regulated activity, drive global compliance.</Standfirst>

The chemicals case centres on the Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals regulation (REACH), enacted in 2007.<Footnote n={17}/> REACH is, in Bradford's account, the paradigmatic instance of economic nondivisibility. The regulation places the burden of proof on manufacturers and importers rather than regulators: under the principle of "no data, no market," firms wishing to place a chemical on the EU market must register it with the European Chemicals Agency, supply safety information, and obtain authorisation for substances of very high concern. The regulation applies not only to new chemicals but, critically, to the tens of thousands of "existing substances" that had been on the European market prior to its enactment — a category that, according to the Commission, accounts for 99 per cent of all chemicals in commerce.

The contrast with the United States' Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA) is stark. The TSCA places the burden of proof on the EPA rather than manufacturers, grandfathers approximately 95 per cent of pre-existing chemicals from systematic safety testing, and imposes substantial evidentiary obstacles to EPA-initiated restrictions on specific substances. The Fifth Circuit's 1991 decision in *Corrosion Proof Fittings v. EPA* — which vacated an EPA rule restricting asbestos on the ground that the agency had not adequately justified the regulation under cost-benefit analysis — is the canonical illustration of how high the U.S. evidentiary bar has been set. The result is that the United States has banned or substantially restricted only a small number of chemicals in the four decades since the TSCA's enactment, while the EU has built a comprehensive registration and authorisation regime covering tens of thousands.

The economic dynamics of REACH compliance follow directly from the structure of the chemical industry. Chemical manufacturing is capital-intensive; production lines are designed around specific input chemistries; reformulation is expensive, requires regulatory testing of its own, and disrupts supply chains. Once a manufacturer has reformulated a product to meet REACH standards — having incurred the registration costs, the safety testing, the supply-chain qualification of REACH-compliant inputs — the marginal cost of selling the reformulated product worldwide is small. The marginal cost of *not* selling it worldwide, by contrast, is the maintenance of a parallel non-compliant production process, with all its associated fixed costs and operational complexity. For most products, in most companies, the calculation is straightforward: comply globally.

Bradford documents the resulting de facto convergence with specific firm-level evidence. Ikea, Lego, and Mattel declared their global production to be PVC-free in response to EU pressure on plasticisers. Dow Chemical announced its entire global production to be REACH-compliant regardless of destination market. Revlon, Unilever, and L'Oréal reformulated their cosmetics product lines to meet REACH standards globally. Estée Lauder confirmed that 95 per cent of its production followed a single REACH-derived safety standard. Foreign chemical manufacturers exporting significant volumes to the EU switched to REACH standards across their production rather than maintain dual lines.<Footnote n={18}/>

The de jure cascade followed. California, in its 2008 Green Chemistry Initiative legislation, explicitly required its Department of Toxic Substances Control to use, "to the maximum extent feasible," safety information generated in other jurisdictions — a provision openly understood at the time as a vehicle for incorporating REACH-derived data into California regulatory decisions. REACH-style frameworks have been adopted in modified form by Canada, Australia, China, South Korea, Japan, and Russia. Each adoption codifies, in domestic law, a regulatory reality that the global chemical industry had already begun to operate under.

The environmental case — built around the 2003 Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive (RoHS) and, in a separate dynamic, the inclusion of aviation in the EU Emissions Trading Scheme — follows the same economic-nondivisibility logic. RoHS regulates the use of hazardous substances in electrical and electronic equipment; its global propagation has produced what Bradford characterises as a fundamental redesign of electronic products worldwide.<Footnote n={19}/> California's 2003 Electronic Waste Recycling Act, again, explicitly incorporated EU standards by reference, including a provision specifying that amendments to the EU directive would automatically flow into California law. Similar RoHS-style frameworks have been adopted in China, Japan, and South Korea. The aviation-ETS case extends the analysis to a domain where the EU explicitly regulates conduct occurring outside European territory — applying emissions obligations to the full journey of any flight departing from or landing at a European airport. The European Court of Justice's 2011 decision in *Air Transport Association of America v. Secretary of State for Energy and Climate Change* upheld the Directive against challenges grounded in customary international law, establishing that the EU's regulatory reach is bounded by the EU's own constitutional commitments rather than by external legal constraints.

</Section>

<Section number="08" title="External constraints, and why <em>markets</em> cannot discipline Brussels">

<Standfirst>The article devotes substantial attention — Part V — to the constraints on the Brussels Effect. The analysis of those constraints is, for a researcher working in regulatory interoperability, perhaps more useful than the affirmative theory, because it identifies the specific structural conditions under which the framework's predictions fail.</Standfirst>

External constraints are weak. Bradford develops this point through three channels of analysis: market constraints, state-level constraints, and constraints arising from international institutions.

Markets cannot discipline the Brussels Effect because the EU primarily regulates inelastic targets. The conventional market response to over-regulation is producer exit: firms relocate to less regulated jurisdictions, capital flows to friendlier shores, and the over-regulating state experiences a costly erosion of its tax base and employment. The Brussels Effect is structurally insulated from this dynamic because the EU's regulatory targets — consumer markets, product safety, competition, data flows — cannot relocate. Consumers do not move to escape product safety rules; data subjects do not emigrate to escape privacy protections; merging firms cannot move the legal transaction to a more permissive jurisdiction. The standard market discipline that constrains over-regulation in elastic domains does not operate in the inelastic domains where the Brussels Effect operates.<Footnote n={20}/>

Other states are similarly limited in their ability to counter the Effect through regulatory action. Bradford develops this point with what is, in retrospect, considerable analytical elegance. A state that disagrees with EU standards has, in principle, three options. It could simply ignore the EU rules and impose its own, accepting that its firms operating in the EU will need to comply with EU rules while its domestic market is governed by its own. This is the status quo for most jurisdictions and produces no race at all — merely parallel regulatory orders with corporate adjustment doing the cross-jurisdictional work. It could compete down — adopt laxer rules to attract regulatory arbitrage. But because the targets are inelastic, this strategy produces no relocation; it merely creates a domestic market governed by laxer rules without any corresponding gain. Or it could compete up — adopt still stricter rules than the EU's. But this strategy is self-defeating: a state that adopts rules stricter than its own preferences would dictate, simply to outflank the EU, ends up with regulatory outcomes worse than its starting position. The outcome is not "outpacing the EU"; the outcome is having even higher and hence less desirable regulatory standards than one would have chosen unilaterally. None of the three strategies disciplines the Effect.

International institutions might be expected to provide a check, but Bradford argues that they do not. The World Trade Organization's jurisdiction does not extend to most of the policy domains where the Brussels Effect operates. Antitrust, privacy, and broad swathes of consumer protection fall outside the WTO's mandate. Even in domains where WTO jurisdiction does reach — sanitary and phytosanitary measures, technical barriers to trade — the WTO's dispute settlement system has been generally unwilling or unable to discipline EU regulatory choices that have global effects. The 1998 *Hormones* case, in which the EU's ban on growth-hormone-treated beef was found inconsistent with WTO rules but the EU declined to lift the ban and instead accepted retaliatory tariffs, illustrates the limit. The WTO can authorise compensation; it cannot compel regulatory change. Other international institutions — the OECD, the World Bank's regulatory programmes, the various sectoral standard-setters — are still less constraining.<Footnote n={21}/>

The cumulative implication is that the Brussels Effect operates in an unusually permissive external environment. There is no market discipline, no effective counter-state action, and no international-institutional check. This is one reason Bradford characterises EU regulatory power as more durable and less easily undermined than traditional forms of power. Military power requires maintenance; sanctions require political will and have known cost structures; economic conditionality depends on the target's vulnerability. Regulatory power, once the conditions are satisfied, runs on the autonomous logic of market adjustment.

</Section>

<Section number="09" title="Internal constraints, and why the framework's <em>limits</em> lie within the EU itself">

<Standfirst>The serious constraints on the Brussels Effect, Bradford argues, are internal to the EU. They are of two kinds, and their interaction with the political economy of EU regulatory expansion has, in the years since the article was written, become a central tension in European politics.</Standfirst>

The first internal constraint is competence-related. The EU's regulatory authority is unevenly distributed across policy domains under the treaties. In areas of exclusive EU competence — the customs union, competition rules necessary for the internal market, common commercial policy, conservation of marine biological resources — the Commission can produce binding rules without member-state co-decision. In areas of shared competence — the internal market more broadly, social policy in defined respects, agriculture and fisheries, environment, consumer protection — the EU may produce binding rules but member states retain authority where the EU has not acted. In areas of supporting competence — culture, education, public health beyond defined respects — the EU's role is limited to coordination and supplementation, and member states retain primary authority. Foreign and security policy, direct taxation, and significant areas of social policy fall outside the integrated regulatory machinery altogether. In domains where the EU as such cannot produce binding rules, Brussels Effects do not arise because there is no Brussels-level rule to propagate.<Footnote n={22}/>

This competence distribution operates as a structural ceiling on the Effect's domain. It also explains the pattern of EU external regulatory power: the domains where the Effect is strongest (competition, data protection, chemicals, environment) are precisely the domains where EU competence is greatest, while the domains where the Effect is weakest or absent (labour markets, direct taxation, foreign policy) are the domains where competence remains with the member states.

The second internal constraint is political-economic, and Bradford treats it as the more important of the two. The political economy that sustains EU regulatory expansion is more fragile than it appears. As the Effect expands and as more domains come under EU regulatory competence, internal divisions among member states intensify. The states whose firms bear disproportionate adjustment costs, whose national traditions favour lighter-touch regulation, or whose political coalitions are less aligned with the precautionary preferences encoded in the EU's regulatory architecture begin to resist further expansion. The expansion of competence and the diffusion of effect are, in the long run, in tension with the political consent that sustains the regulatory machinery in the first place. Bradford treats this tension as the most significant constraint on the framework: not because it stops the Effect from operating in its current domains, but because it limits the further expansion of those domains.

This second internal constraint has aged into a more important observation than it may have appeared in 2012. The political economy of EU regulatory expansion — the question of whether member states will continue to accept the costs of stricter common standards as the Brussels Effect comes to define more of global commerce, and the question of whether the populations of those states will continue to consent to a regulatory expansion they have only indirectly authorised — is now a central tension in the politics of every major EU regulatory initiative. The AI Act passed only with significant internal disagreement among member states about the appropriate stringency of obligations on foundation models; the negotiations on the Data Act and the Digital Services Act exposed similar divisions; the broader Green Deal regulatory package has become a flashpoint in domestic European political contests, particularly in member states where the right has organised around resistance to what is characterised as Brussels regulatory overreach.

The article also notes structural constraints on the *kinds* of standards the Effect can globalise. Labour standards, as already discussed, are divisible: a firm operating in Europe must follow European labour rules but can simultaneously employ workers in less protective jurisdictions under quite different terms. Financial transactions are elastic: a financial transactions tax, were the EU to impose one, would simply divert trading activity to non-EU centres. Tax rates more broadly are elastic in respect of legal incorporation if not always in respect of physical investment. These domains mark the structural edges of the framework. They also identify the regulatory choices that the EU could not propagate globally even if it wished to — a fact that, Bradford observes, shapes the EU's choice of which regulatory ambitions to pursue.

</Section>

<Section number="10" title="The <em>relevance</em> for AI governance and for the doctoral question">

<Standfirst>Bradford does not discuss AI governance — the AI Act would not be tabled for another nine years — but the framework she developed in 2012 is now the dominant lens through which scholars and policy-makers analyse the global reach of EU AI regulation. The fit is imperfect, and the imperfections matter for the specific question of regulatory interoperability between the EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework.</Standfirst>

The AI Act, in force from 2024, was explicitly designed in the shadow of the Brussels Effect. Its drafters anticipated, and the secondary literature has largely concluded, that the Act will project EU standards onto AI systems deployed globally through the same mechanisms Bradford identified for the GDPR.<Footnote n={23}/> A frontier model trained for deployment in the EU must satisfy EU obligations on documentation, transparency, risk assessment, human oversight, and — for general-purpose AI models with systemic risk — additional obligations on adversarial testing, incident reporting, and cybersecurity. The architectural and economic costs of maintaining a parallel non-EU-compliant version of the same model are, for most providers, prohibitive: a foundation model is a single training artefact; the documentation and evaluation work that produces it is upstream of any deployment decision; the technical infrastructure that serves it is, in practice, unified across jurisdictions for cost reasons. The de facto cascade should follow.

Whether it will, however, depends on the precise satisfaction of all five conditions, and the AI case strains several of them.

**Market power**: the EU remains a major market for AI services, but its share of global AI demand is smaller than its share of, say, chemical demand. The leading frontier developers are concentrated in the United States and increasingly in China; their commercial incentives to comply with EU rules depend on the EU's share of their revenue and the political-strategic value of the European market. For some developers — Microsoft, Google, Meta — the EU is large enough that compliance is the dominant strategy. For others — particularly developers serving primarily domestic or non-European markets — withdrawal from the EU may be a credible alternative. The 2024-2025 wave of model withholdings, in which several major U.S. developers declined to release particular features or models in the EU rather than comply with specific obligations, signals that the elasticity of producer participation is higher in AI than in chemicals.

**Regulatory capacity**: the AI Act's enforcement architecture is novel, distributed between the Commission (with primary authority over general-purpose AI models) and national authorities (with primary authority over high-risk systems deployed in their territory), and as yet untested. The European AI Office, established within the Commission, is staffing up; the national competent authorities are at varying stages of readiness; the coordination mechanisms among them are still being designed. The robustness of the capacity condition will depend on the actual operational record of these institutions over the next several years.

**Preference for strict rules**: present, but contested internally. The Act passed with significant disagreement among member states about its stringency. France, with its frontier-developer Mistral, lobbied hard for lighter obligations on general-purpose AI models, and the eventual text reflects a compromise that some industry observers and critics regard as more permissive than the Commission's original proposal. The political economy of implementation, particularly around the systemic-risk threshold and the codes of practice that operationalise compliance, is genuinely fragile and could shift further toward leniency under sustained industry pressure.

**Inelastic targets**: AI services are partly inelastic (consumers do not relocate) but partly elastic (developers can, and increasingly do, withhold products from the EU market rather than comply). The combination of inelastic users and elastic providers produces a regulatory dynamic distinct from the chemical or merger cases, where both sides are largely fixed in place.

**Nondivisibility**: holds for some systems (foundation models, large language models trained on a single architecture) and not for others (jurisdictionally segmented deployments are technically feasible for many narrower applications). The post-deployment compliance landscape may turn out to be more divisible than the training-time obligations, with some developers maintaining EU-compliant deployment endpoints distinct from non-EU endpoints. The empirical record on this point is still developing.

The cumulative effect is that the Brussels Effect's operation in the AI domain is probable but not foreordained. Bradford's framework predicts a strong cascade; the structural features of AI as a regulated object suggest the cascade may be weaker, more contested, and more selectively applied than in the GDPR or REACH cases. This is the analytical horizon against which the doctoral question on regulatory interoperability between the EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework must be posed.

The interoperability question, in this framing, is not merely a technical question of how two regulatory documents map onto each other. It is a question about which side bears the burden of adjustment. If the Brussels Effect operates fully in the AI domain, interoperability becomes a question of how American institutions — including federal agencies operating under NIST guidance — accommodate the European standard, with the U.S. soft-law instrument effectively codifying compliance practices already being followed in private-sector implementations. If the Brussels Effect operates only partially, interoperability becomes a more genuinely bilateral problem of mapping two distinct regulatory architectures onto a shared technological substrate, with neither side dominant and both sides retaining substantive autonomy. Which of these scenarios obtains will depend on the empirical evolution of the five conditions over the next several years — and the doctoral research that examines this evolution will, of necessity, take Bradford's 2012 framework as its analytical instrument.

</Section>

<Section number="11" title="Coda">

What remains theoretically robust in Bradford's argument is the five-condition framework itself. Fifteen years of subsequent scholarship has tested it across new domains — financial regulation (where it does not operate, confirming the elasticity condition), data protection (where it operates almost completely, with the GDPR providing the canonical empirical case), content moderation (where it operates with significant friction, particularly around free-expression conflicts with the U.S. First Amendment), and now artificial intelligence (where the answer is still being written). The framework has held up across this empirical expansion, and the analytical move that gave the article its lasting importance — the disaggregation of the de facto and de jure mechanisms, and the identification of nondivisibility as the operative conversion factor — has become canonical in the international-regulatory-power literature.

What has aged is the article's implicit normative posture. Bradford in 2012 wrote in a register that was, if not celebratory, at least curious and admiring — describing a form of European power that had been overlooked, was real, and was producing measurable global effects in domains where international institutions had failed. The decade and a half since has complicated that posture. The Brussels Effect is now visible enough to its targets to provoke counter-mobilisation. In the United States, the response has taken the form of state-level data-protection laws that diverge from GDPR templates in ways designed to assert American regulatory autonomy; federal legislative resistance to importing EU-style rules in domains such as platform regulation and AI safety; and a growing body of legal scholarship arguing that the Brussels Effect operates as a form of regulatory imperialism deserving political pushback. In China, the response has taken the form of an explicit Chinese alternative to both American and European digital governance models — an explicit articulation of a third path organised around state interests rather than market or rights. In major developing economies, the pattern is one of selective adoption: incorporation of EU standards in domains where domestic interests align, combined with active resistance in domains where the political cost is higher than the regulatory benefit.

The framework's predictions for the AI era thus depend on a second-order question that Bradford did not need to answer in 2012 but that doctoral work on the subject now cannot avoid: what happens to unilateral regulatory globalisation when its targets recognise the mechanism, learn to anticipate it, and begin to build institutions designed to resist it. The five conditions remain individually necessary and jointly sufficient. But the political conditions for those conditions to keep holding may be more fragile than they were when the article first appeared. The Brussels Effect may persist, but in a more contested form, with weaker propagation in newer domains, and with the EU's own internal political economy increasingly stressed by the costs of sustaining the regulatory machinery that produces the Effect.

The reader is left, in the end, with a clearer view of what the framework is and what it is not. It is a theoretically rigorous account of a real mechanism of international regulatory order, supported by carefully selected empirical cases and disciplined by an explicit specification of necessary and sufficient conditions. It is not a prediction that EU regulatory hegemony will persist indefinitely, nor a normative argument that it should. It is an analytical instrument — and for the doctoral researcher working on regulatory interoperability between the EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, it is the analytical instrument with which the central questions of the field must be posed. Whether those questions can be fully answered with this instrument alone is itself a doctoral question worth asking.

</Section>
